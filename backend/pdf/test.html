*557 MR. JUSTICE BRENNAN delivered the opinion of the Court.

The issue for decision in this case is whether the Truth in Lending Act (TILA), 82 Stat. 146, as

amended, 15 U.S. C. § 1601 et seq., requires that the existence of an acceleraƟon clause always be

disclosed on the face of a credit agreement. The Federal Reserve Board staﬀ has consistently

construed the statute and regulaƟons as imposing no such uniform requirement. Because we believe

that a high degree of deference to this administraƟve interpretaƟon is warranted, we hold that TILA

does not mandate a general rule of disclosure for acceleraƟon clauses.

I

The several respondents in this case purchased automobiles from various dealers, ﬁnancing their

purchases through standard retail installment contracts that were assigned to peƟƟoner Ford Motor

Credit Co. (FMCC), a ﬁnance company. Each contract provided that respondents were to pay a pre-

computed ﬁnance charge. As required by TILA and Federal Reserve Board RegulaƟon Z, which

implements the Act, the front page of each contract disclosed and explained certain features of the

agreement. See 15 U.S. C. § 1631; 12 CFR § 226.6 (a) (1979). Among these disclosures was a

paragraph informing the buyer that he

"may prepay his obligaƟons under this contract in full at any Ɵme prior to maturity of the ﬁnal

instalment here-under, and, if he does so, shall receive a rebate of the unearned porƟon of the

Finance Charge computed under the sum of the digits method. . . ."

The face of the contract also stated that temporary default on a parƟcular installment would result in

a predetermined *558 delinquency charge. Not menƟoned on the disclosure page was a clause in

the body of the contract giving the creditor a right to accelerate payment of the enƟre debt upon the

buyer's default.[1]

Respondents subsequently commenced four separate suits against FMCC in the United States District

Court for the District of Oregon, alleging, inter alia, that FMCC had violated TILA and RegulaƟon Z by

failing to disclose on the front page of the contract that the creditor retained the right to accelerate

payment of the debt.[2] In two of the suits,[3] the District Court held that facial disclosure of the

acceleraƟon clauses was mandated by the provision of TILA that compels publicaƟon of "default,

delinquency, or similar charges payable in the event of late payments," 15 U.S. C. §§ 1638 (a) (9),

1639 (a) (7). App. 30-31, 37, 69-71. Respondents in the other two acƟons prevailed on diﬀerent

grounds.[4] All four cases were consolidated on appeal to the Ninth Circuit.

The Court of Appeals agreed with the District Court that TILA imposes a general acceleraƟon-clause

disclosure requirement.[5] Rather than resƟng on the District Court's holding that acceleraƟon is a

default charge, however, the Court of Appeals based its decision on the narrower principle that

under RegulaƟon Z "[t]he creditor must disclose whether a rebate of unearned interest will be made

upon acceleraƟon *559 and also disclose the method by which the amount of unearned interest will

be computed if the debt is accelerated." 588 F.2d 753, 757 (1978), quoƟng St. Germain v. Bank of

Hawaii, 573 F.2d 572, 577 (CA9 1977). See 12 CFR § 226.8 (b) (7) (1979). Implicit in the conclusion of

the Court of Appeals  and explicit in its preceding St. Germain decision  was the rejecƟon of a

contrary administraƟve interpretaƟon of the perƟnent statutory and regulatory provisions. In

adopƟng its parƟcular approach, the Court of Appeals mapped a path through the disclosure thicket

that diverges from the routes traveled by the Courts of Appeals for several other Circuits.[6] We

granted cerƟorari, 442 U.S. 940 (1979), to resolve the conﬂict. We reverse.

II

The Truth in Lending Act has the broad purpose of promoƟng "the informed use of credit" by

assuring "meaningful disclosure of credit terms" to consumers. 15 U.S. C. § 1601. Because of their

complexity and variety, however, credit transacƟons defy exhausƟve regulaƟon by a single statute.

Congress therefore delegated expansive authority to *560 the Federal Reserve Board to elaborate

and expand the legal framework governing commerce in credit. 15 U.S. C. § 1604; Mourning v. Family

PublicaƟons Service, Inc., 411 U.S. 356 (1973). The Board executed its responsibility by promulgaƟng

RegulaƟon Z, 12 CFR Part 226 (1979), which at least partly ﬁlls the statutory gaps. Even RegulaƟon Z,

however, cannot speak explicitly to every credit disclosure issue. At the threshold, therefore,

interpretaƟon of TILA and RegulaƟon Z demands an examinaƟon of their express language; absent a

clear expression, it becomes necessary to consider the implicit character of the statutory scheme.

For the reasons following we conclude that the issue of acceleraƟon disclosure is not governed by

clear expression in the statute or regulaƟon, and that it is appropriate to defer to the Federal Reserve

Board and staﬀ in determining what resoluƟon of that issue is implied by the truth-in-lending

enactments.

Respondents have advanced two theories to buƩress their claim that the Act and regulaƟon

expressly mandate disclosure of acceleraƟon clauses. In the District Court, they contended that

acceleraƟon clauses were comprehended by the general statutory prescripƟon that a creditor shall

disclose "default, delinquency, or similar charges payable in the event of late payments," 15 U.S. C.

§§ 1638 (a) (9), 1639 (a) (7), and were included within the provision of RegulaƟon Z requiring

disclosure of the "amount, or method of compuƟng the amount, of any default, delinquency, or

similar charges payable in the event of late payments," 12 CFR § 226.8 (b) (4) (1979). Before this

Court, respondents follow the Court of Appeals in arguing that 12 CFR § 226.8 (b) (7) may be the

source of an obligaƟon to disclose procedures governing the rebate of unearned ﬁnance charges that

accrue under acceleraƟon. That secƟon commands

"[i]denƟﬁcaƟon of the method of compuƟng any unearned porƟon of the ﬁnance charge in the event

of prepayment in full of an obligaƟon which includes precomputed *561 ﬁnance charges and a

statement of the amount or method of computaƟon of any charge that may be deducted from the

amount of any rebate of such unearned ﬁnance charge that will be credited to an obligaƟon or

refunded to the customer."

A fair reading of the perƟnent provisions does not sustain respondents' contenƟon that acceleraƟon

clauses are within their terms.

An acceleraƟon clause cannot be equated with a "default, delinquency, or similar charg[e]," subject

to disclosure under 15 U.S. C. §§ 1638 (a) (9), 1639 (a) (7), and 12 CFR § 226.8 (b) (4). The prerogaƟve

of acceleraƟon aﬀords the creditor a mechanism for collecƟng the outstanding porƟon of a debt on

which there has been a parƟal default. In itself, acceleraƟon entails no monetary penalty, although a

creditor may independently impose such a penalty, for example, by failing to rebate unearned

ﬁnance charges. A "default, delinquency, or similar charg[e]," on the other hand, self-evidently refers

to a speciﬁc assessable sum. Thus, within the trade, delinquency charges are understood to be

"the compensaƟon a creditor receives on a precomputed contract for the debtor's delay in making

Ɵmely instalment payments," 1 CCH Consumer Credit Guide ¶¶ 4230, 4231 (1977) (emphasis added).

AcceleraƟon is not compensatory; a creditor accelerates to avoid further delay by demanding

immediate payment of the outstanding debt. See id., ¶ 4231; Uniform Consumer Credit Code of

1968, § 2.203, oﬃcial comment 2, 7 U. L. A. 315-316 (1978); § 2.204 (3), id., at 317.

The language employed in TILA §§ 1638 (a) (9) and 1639 (a) (7), and in 12 CFR § 226.8 (b) (4) (1979),

conﬁrms the interpretaƟon of "charges" as speciﬁc penalty sums. The statutory provisions speak of

"charges payable in the event of late payments." (Emphasis added.) Even if one considers the

burdensomeness of acceleraƟon as a form of "charge" upon the debtor, it would hardly make sense

to speak of *562 that burden as "payable" to the creditor. Similarly RegulaƟon Z orders disclosure of

the "amount, or method of compuƟng the amount, of any default, delinquency, or similar charges. . .

." (Emphasis added.) That command has no sensible applicaƟon to the remedy of acceleraƟon. In

short, we would have to stretch these provisions beyond their obvious limits to construe them as a

mandate for the disclosure of acceleraƟon clauses.[7]

The prepayment rebate disclosure regulaƟon, 12 CFR § 226.8 (b) (7) (1979), also fails to aﬀord direct

support for an invariable speciﬁc acceleraƟon disclosure rule. To be sure, payment by the debtor in

response to acceleraƟon might be deemed a prepayment within the ambit of that regulaƟon. But so

long as the creditor's rebate pracƟce under acceleraƟon is idenƟcal to its policy with respect to

voluntary prepayments, separate disclosure of the acceleraƟon policy does not seem obligatory

under a literal reading of the regulaƟon. SecƟon 226.8 (b) (7), therefore, squares with the posiƟon of

the Federal Reserve Board staﬀ that speciﬁc disclosure of acceleraƟon rebate policy is only necessary

when that policy varies from the custom with respect to voluntary prepayment rebates. FRB Oﬃcial

Staﬀ InterpretaƟon No. FC-0054, 12 CFR Part 226 Appendix, p. 627 (1979).

III

Notwithstanding the absence of an express statutory mandate that acceleraƟon procedures be

invariably disclosed, the *563 Court of Appeals has held that the "creditor must [always] disclose

whether a rebate of unearned interest will be made upon acceleraƟon and also disclose the method

by which the amount of unearned interest will be computed if the debt is accelerated." St.

Germain v. Bank of Hawaii, 573 F. 2d, at 577; accord, 588 F.2d, at 757-758. In so deciding, the Court

of Appeals in St. Germain explicitly rejected the view of the Federal Reserve Board staﬀ that the right

of acceleraƟon need not be disclosed, and that rebate pracƟce under acceleraƟon must be disclosed

only if it diﬀers from the creditor's rebate policy with respect to voluntary prepayment. FRB Oﬃcial

Staﬀ InterpretaƟon No. FC-0054, supra; see FRB Public InformaƟon LeƩer No. 851, [1974-1977

Transfer Binder] CCH Consumer Credit Guide ¶ 31, 173; FRB Public InformaƟon LeƩer No. 1208, id., ¶

31,647; FRB Public InformaƟon LeƩer No. 1324, 5 CCH Consumer Credit Guide ¶ 31,827

(1979).[8] Rather, St. Germain declared that it would *564 "choose the direcƟon that makes more

sense to us in trying to achieve the congressional purpose of providing meaningful disclosure to the

debtor about the costs of his borrowing." 573 F.2d, at 576-577.

*565 It is a commonplace that courts will further legislaƟve goals by ﬁlling the intersƟƟal silences

within a statute or a regulaƟon. Because legislators cannot foresee all eventualiƟes, judges must

decide unanƟcipated cases by extrapolaƟng from related statutes or administraƟve provisions. But

legislaƟve silence is not always the result of a lack of prescience; it may instead betoken permission

or, perhaps, considered abstenƟon from regulaƟon. In that event, judges are not accredited to

supersede Congress or the appropriate agency by embellishing upon the regulatory scheme.

Accordingly, cauƟon must temper judicial creaƟvity in the face of legislaƟve or regulatory silence.

At the very least, that cauƟon requires aƩenƟveness to the views of the administraƟve enƟty

appointed to apply and enforce a statute. And deference is especially appropriate in the process of

interpreƟng the Truth in Lending Act and RegulaƟon Z. Unless demonstrably irraƟonal, Federal

Reserve Board staﬀ opinions construing the Act or RegulaƟon should be disposiƟve for several

reasons.

*566 The Court has oŌen repeated the general proposiƟon that considerable respect is due "`the

interpretaƟon given [a] statute by the oﬃcers or agency charged with its administraƟon.'" Zenith

Radio Corp. v. United States, 437 U.S. 443, 450 (1978), quoƟng Udall v. Tallman, 380 U.S. 1, 16 (1965);

see, e. g., Power Reactor Co. v. Electricians, 367 U.S. 396, 408 (1961). An agency's construcƟon of its

own regulaƟons has been regarded as especially due that respect. See Bowles v. Seminole Rock

Co., 325 U.S. 410, 413-414 (1945). This tradiƟonal acquiescence in administraƟve experƟse is

parƟcularly apt under TILA, because the Federal Reserve Board has played a pivotal role in "seƫng

[the statutory] machinery in moƟon. . . ." Norwegian Nitrogen Products Co. v. United

States, 288 U.S. 294, 315 (1933). As we emphasized in Mourning v. Family PublicaƟons Service,

Inc., 411 U.S. 356 (1973), Congress delegated broad administraƟve lawmaking power to the Federal

Reserve Board when it framed TILA. The Act is best construed by those who gave it substance in

promulgaƟng regulaƟons thereunder.[9]

Furthermore, Congress has speciﬁcally designated the Federal Reserve Board and staﬀ as the primary

source for interpretaƟon and applicaƟon of truth-in-lending law. Because creditors need sure

guidance through the "highly technical" Truth in Lending Act, S. Rep. No. 93-278, p. 13 (1973),

legislators have twice acted to promote reliance upon Federal Reserve pronouncements. In 1974,

TILA was amended to *567 provide creditors with a defense from liability based upon good-faith

compliance with a "rule, regulaƟon, or interpretaƟon" of the Federal Reserve Board itself. § 406, 88

Stat. 1518, codiﬁed at 15 U.S. C. § 1640 (f). The explicit purpose of the amendment was to relieve the

creditor of the burden of choosing "between the Board's construcƟon of the Act and the creditor's

own assessment of how a court may interpret the Act." S. Rep. No. 93-278, supra, at 13. The same

raƟonale prompted a further change in the statute in 1976, authorizing a liability defense for

"conformity with any interpretaƟon or approval by an oﬃcial or employee of the Federal Reserve

System duly authorized by the Board to issue such interpretaƟons or approvals. . . ." § 3 (b), 90 Stat.

197, codiﬁed at 15 U.S. C. § 1640 (f); see 122 Cong. Rec. 2836 (1976) (remarks of Sen. Garn); id., at

2852 (remarks of Rep. Annunzio, chairman of Consumer Aﬀairs SubcommiƩee); ibid. (remarks of

Rep. Rousselot); 121 Cong. Rec. 36927 (1975) (remarks of Rep. Annunzio); id., at 36927-36928

(remarks of Rep. Wylie).[10]

The enactment and expansion of § 1640 (f) has signiﬁcance beyond the express creaƟon of a good-

faith immunity.[11] That statutory provision signals an unmistakable congressional decision to treat

administraƟve rulemaking and interpretaƟon *568 under TILA as authoritaƟve. Moreover, language

in the legislaƟve history evinces a decided preference for resolving interpreƟve issues by uniform

administraƟve decision, rather than piecemeal through liƟgaƟon.[12] See S. Rep. No. 93-278, supra, at

13-14; 122 Cong. Rec. 2852 (1976) (remarks of Rep. Annunzio); 121 Cong. Rec. 36927 (1975)

(remarks of Rep. Annunzio). Courts should honor that congressional choice. Thus, while not

abdicaƟng their ulƟmate judicial responsibility to determine the law, cf. generally SEC v. Chenery

Corp., 318 U.S. 80, 92-94 (1943), judges ought to refrain from subsƟtuƟng their own intersƟƟal

lawmaking for that of the Federal Reserve, so long as the laƩer's lawmaking is not irraƟonal.

Finally, wholly apart from jurisprudenƟal consideraƟons or congressional intent, deference to the

Federal Reserve is compelled by necessity; a court that tries to chart a true course to the Act's

purpose embarks upon a voyage without a compass when it disregards the agency's views. The

concept of "meaningful disclosure" that animates TILA, see St. Germain, 573 F. 2d, at 577, cannot be

applied in the abstract. Meaningful disclosure does not mean more disclosure. Rather, it describes a

balance between "compeƟng consideraƟons of complete disclosure . . . and the need to avoid . . .

[informaƟonal overload]." S. Rep 96-73, p. 3 (1979) (accompanying S. 108, Truth in Lending

SimpliﬁcaƟon and Reform Act); see S. Rep. No. 95-720, pp. 2-3 (1978); 63 Federal Reserve Board Ann.

Rep. 326, 349-350 (1976); Comment. AcceleraƟon Clause Disclosure Under the Truth in Lending Act,

77 Colum. L. Rev. 649, 662-663 (1977). And striking the appropriate balance is an empirical process

that entails invesƟgaƟon into consumer psychology and that presupposes *569 broad experience

with credit pracƟces. AdministraƟve agencies are simply beƩer suited than courts to engage in such a

process.

The Federal Reserve Board staﬀ treatment of acceleraƟon disclosure raƟonally accommodates the

conﬂicƟng demands for completeness and for simplicity. In determining that acceleraƟon rebate

pracƟces need be disclosed only when they diverge from other prepayment rebate pracƟces, the

Federal Reserve has adopted what may be termed a "boƩom-line" approach: that the most

important informaƟon in a credit purchase is that which explains diﬀering net charges and rates. Cf.

S. Rep. No. 96-73, supra, at 3-4; 63 Federal Reserve Board Ann. Rep., supra, at 350-352. Although the

staﬀ might have decided that acceleraƟon rebates are so analyƟcally disƟnct from idenƟcal voluntary

prepayment rebates as to warrant separate disclosure, it was reasonable to conclude, alternaƟvely,

that ordinary consumers would be concerned chieﬂy about diﬀering ﬁnancial

consequences.[13]*570 Faced with an apparent lacuna in the express prescripƟons of TILA and

RegulaƟon Z, the Court of Appeals had no ground for displacing the Federal Reserve staﬀ's expert

judgment.

Accordingly, we decide that the Court of Appeals erred in rejecƟng the views of the Federal Reserve

Board and staﬀ, and holding that separate disclosure of acceleraƟon rebate pracƟces is always

required.[14]

Reversed and remanded.

MR. JUSTICE BLACKMUN, with whom THE CHIEF JUSTICE joins, concurring.

I join the Court's opinion but write separately because I do not fully agree with the statement in note

13 of the opinion, ante, at 569, that the Federal Reserve Board's approach to the disclosure of

acceleraƟon rebates is "equally logical" with other alternaƟves it might have chosen. In parƟcular, I

am concerned that the Board's emphasis on a creditor's rebate policy rather than its

contract rights steers the Truth in Lending Act away from the moorings of contract law in a manner

that may not prove salutary for the welfare of consumers of ﬁnancial credit.

To be sure, consumers contemplaƟng installment purchases are concerned with the "boƩom

line," ante, at 569, of how much they will be required to pay. But there is liƩle doubt, in my view, that

consumers who read the required disclosures *571 think that they are reading a descripƟon of their

legal rights and obligaƟons, and not merely an explanaƟon of "pracƟces" or "policies" of the creditor

that may be changed to their detriment at the creditor's will. Although there may be reason to

believe that a major ﬁnance company, such as Ford Motor Credit Co., will adhere to its rebate

pracƟces despite the legal right to demand more upon acceleraƟon than it said it would, I am not

sanguine that a less responsible organizaƟon always will do the same. The result could be confusion

and unanƟcipated ﬁnancial loss, as well as fruitless liƟgaƟon.

UlƟmately, I think the interpretaƟon adopted by the FiŌh Circuit in McDaniel v. Fulton Nat.

Bank, 571 F.2d 948 (en banc), clariﬁed, 576 F.2d 1156 (1978) (en banc), which requires disclosure of

the creditor's right to retain ﬁnance charges upon acceleraƟon when it diﬀers from the right to such

charges upon prepayment, may prove to be a sounder and more durable applicaƟon of the statute

than the posiƟon currently adopted by the Board. Nevertheless, I agree with the Court that the

Board's approach is reasonable. In order to uphold the Board's posiƟon, "we need not ﬁnd that its

construcƟon is the only reasonable one, or even that it is the result we would have reached had the

quesƟon arisen in the ﬁrst instance in judicial proceedings." Udall v. Tallman, 380 U.S. 1, 16 (1965),

quoƟng Unemployment Comm'n v. Aragon, 329 U.S. 143, 153 (1946). Accordingly, I agree that the

courts should not add to the disclosure obligaƟons that the Board has outlined through its staﬀ

opinions.
